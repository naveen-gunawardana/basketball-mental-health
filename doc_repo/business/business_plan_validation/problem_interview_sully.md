## 5. Explore Coach's Worldview (15 min)

*Go deep on the top 1-2 problems ranked highest*

### Problem A: Role Confusion / Position Changes

**"What does this look like when it happens?"**
"Leo was growing up as a not a big man, but then he grew tall so we kind of made him into big man." Also talked to Joe about shooting more instead of just taking it to the basket; same with Story and Daniel shooting more.

**"How do you handle it right now?"**
Individual conversations with players. Normalizes it: "No one really ever figures out as a basketball player, like you're constantly changing, you're constantly getting injured or getting stronger or getting taller."

**"What's frustrating about that approach?"**
Not explicitly stated, but implied it's ongoing/constant work.

**"How often do you deal with this?"**
- ☐ Daily ☒ Weekly ☐ Occasionally

"Minutes is all the time. Role confusion a little bit."

**"What's the cost — to the player, to the team, to you?"**
Players can get frustrated. Leo's transition from small forward to center has been "hard for him."

---

### Problem B: Team Culture — Trash Talk vs. Hype Balance

**"What does this look like when it happens?"**
"Specifically like practices... definitely some kids are trash talking, but then not saying anything positive... I think we've gotta have both if you're gonna be a good team."

**"How do you handle it right now?"**
Observes it, but notes it's evolved: "Over the course of this season, I have kind of seen a trend of more trash talk, less [hype]." Rationalizes it as players getting closer — "that trash talk is like friendly... you're trash talking your friends."

**"What's frustrating about that approach?"**
"Trash talk is fine, but then there's not enough hype as well." Games have hype (because winning 20-40 points), but practice lacks it.

**"How often do you deal with this?"**
- ☒ Daily ☐ Weekly ☐ Occasionally

**"What's the cost — to the player, to the team, to you?"**
Some players (like Leo) internalize negative comments after mistakes.

**Response:**
Age proximity matters. He's seen as "in between a coach and a player" by players like Leo. This makes communication easier.

## 7. Dig Deeper — Mentorship

*Test whether coaches would value external mentorship for players*

> "Have you ever thought about connecting your players with older players — like college guys — to talk through stuff?"

**Response:**
Has personal experience with this. His mentors:
- **Jackie** — "Definitely" a mentor, outside of UHS coaches
- **His brother** — 6'6" point guard, trained him to become a point guard/shooter after injury: "I'm going to train you... you're going to become a point guard shooter"
- **Physical therapist/trainer** — "Shot doctor," fixed his shot, also helped with mental stuff during injury

> "If someone set up a mentorship program where your players could have weekly check-ins with a college/pro player, would that be useful? Would you recommend it?"

**Response:**
"I do think it's a great idea." But with a critical caveat (see below).

> "What would you want them to work on in those conversations?"

**Response:**
Not just basketball skills — life beyond basketball. "Basketball was my life, but they needed to help you with everything else in my life more than they were helping me with basketball because I was going to help myself with basketball."

> "What would make you hesitant about something like that?"

**Response:**
**CRITICAL INSIGHT**: Had a sports psychologist freshman year. Didn't work well.

"The one flaw I would say is over time, I would at least stop thinking for myself... when I would talk to my sports psychologist, it felt like I was kind of just talking to him rather than thinking through stuff with him, like we were just... empty space, empty conversations."

"That's very different from like the brand of people who ended up being my mentors... I really expected him to be my mentor, but all the people who I feel like the brand of mentors [were organic relationships]."

**Key takeaway**: Formal psych felt like empty talking. Organic mentors (brother, trainer, Jackie) were effective because they were thinking WITH him, not just listening.


---

## 8. Practical Fit

*Understand what it takes for something to work for his team*

> "If there was a tool or program to help with the mental side — what would it need to look like for you to actually use it with your team?"

**Response:**
Needs to be collaborative, not one-way. Must avoid the "empty conversations" problem he had with his sports psychologist. Should be thinking WITH the player, not just listening.

> "What's tried before that didn't work? What made it fail?"

**Response:**
Sports psychologist freshman year. Failed because it felt like "just talking to him rather than thinking through stuff with him." The conversations felt "empty."

In contrast, organic mentors (brother, trainer, Jackie) worked because:
- They were training/working on skills together
- They helped with life beyond basketball, not just basketball
- It was a two-way relationship, not just venting

> "Who makes the decision to bring something like this to the team — you, head coach, parents, AD?"

**Response:**
"I'm obviously like the young guy in the program, so I'm not going to be the guy who's going to connect you to everyone." Suggested talking to:
- All the coaches
- Randy, Alex — "the old heads" who can connect to everyone
- Sports psych at UHS
