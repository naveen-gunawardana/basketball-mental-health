# Player-Coach Meeting Script

## Purpose
Help players have productive conversations with coaches about playing time, development, and role. Based on interview insights — only 3/16 players know how to advocate effectively, but those who do (like Daniel) see results.

---

## The Problem We're Solving

**What coaches see** (from Tim):
> "All the students ask about playing time because they want to know what they can do. But only three know how to advocate for themselves and actually say what they can do to show or try to get something out the deal."

**What works** (Daniel's example):
- Asked about playing time
- Engaged with what he could do to earn it
- "Actually stepped it up a lot" and "been cooking lately"

**What doesn't work**:
- Just asking "why don't I play more?"
- Accepting the answer passively without engaging
- Not following up with action

---

## Before the Meeting

### Player Prep (5-10 min)

**1. Self-Assessment**
Answer honestly before you talk to coach:

| Question | Your Answer |
|----------|-------------|
| What do I do well in practice? | |
| What do I struggle with in games that I can do in practice? | |
| What's one specific thing I want to improve? | |
| What do I think my role on the team is? | |
| What do I want my role to be? | |

**2. Pick Your Focus**
Don't bring 10 things. Pick ONE:
- [ ] Understanding my role better
- [ ] Getting feedback on what to work on
- [ ] Understanding why I'm getting certain minutes
- [ ] Specific skill development
- [ ] How to translate practice to games

**3. Come With Evidence**
Think of 1-2 specific examples:
- A practice moment where you did something well
- A game moment where you held back or struggled
- Something you've been working on

## The Meeting Script

### Opening (30 sec)
**Don't**: "Coach, why don't I play more?"  
**Do**: "Coach, I want to get better and help the team more. Can I get your feedback on what I should focus on?"

**Example openers**:
- "Hey Coach, I've been thinking about my game. Can I ask you a couple things?"
- "Coach, I want to earn more minutes. What do I need to show you?"
- "I feel like I can do more than I'm showing in games. Can you help me figure out what's holding me back?"

---

### The Core Conversation (5-10 min)

**Step 1: Ask for honest feedback**
> "What's one thing I need to work on most to help the team?"

*Listen. Don't defend. Take notes if you want.*

**Step 2: Ask about the gap (if relevant)**
> "I feel like I can do [X] in practice but I'm not showing it in games. What do you think is causing that?"

*This shows self-awareness. Tim specifically mentioned this as the #1 issue.*

**Step 3: Ask what "earning it" looks like**
> "What would I need to show you in practice to earn more playing time?"

*This is the key question. You're asking for the criteria, not complaining about the outcome.*

**Step 4: Propose your commitment**
> "If I focus on [specific thing], can we check in again in [2 weeks]?"

*This shows you're serious and gives a follow-up.*

---

### Closing (30 sec)

**Thank them**:
> "Thanks Coach. I appreciate the honesty. I'm going to work on [X]."

**Confirm next step**:
> "Can I check back with you in a couple weeks to see how I'm doing?"

---

## After the Meeting

### Immediate (same day)
- [ ] Write down what coach said (don't trust memory)
- [ ] Identify the ONE thing to focus on
- [ ] Tell yourself: "This is my focus for the next 2 weeks"

### Next 2 Weeks
- [ ] Work on the specific thing coach mentioned
- [ ] In practice, actively try to demonstrate it
- [ ] Don't be afraid to make mistakes (Tim: "Get out here and make as many mistakes as possible")

### Follow-Up Meeting
- [ ] Come back with evidence: "I've been working on [X]. Here's what I've tried."
- [ ] Ask: "Have you noticed any improvement? What else should I focus on?"

## Example: Jet Talking to Tim

### Jet's Situation
- High motivation (9-10)
- Confidence gap: can do things in practice, hesitates in games
- Gets hard on himself after misses
- Knows he plays different against friends vs. strangers

### How It Could Go

**Jet**: "Coach, I want to ask you something. I feel like I can do more than I'm showing in games. In practice I feel good, but in games I hesitate. What do you think I should focus on?"

**Tim**: "Jet, I've seen you do things in practice that you don't even try in games. What's going through your head?"

**Jet**: "Honestly, I get in my own head. If I miss a shot early, I stop being aggressive."

**Tim**: "That's exactly it. Here's what I want you to do — next game, I don't care if you miss. I want you to stay aggressive. Take the shots you'd take in practice. I'm not going to pull you for missing."

**Jet**: "Okay. So even if I miss early, keep attacking?"

**Tim**: "Yes. The only way you'll get past it is by playing through it. I'd rather see you miss being aggressive than see you pass up good looks."

**Jet**: "Got it. Can I check in with you after next game to see how I did?"

**Tim**: "Yeah, let's talk after Wednesday's game."

## What NOT to Do

| Don't | Why |
|-------|-----|
| "Why don't I play more?" | Sounds like complaining, not seeking improvement |
| "I'm better than [other player]" | Comparing to teammates puts coach on defense |
| "My parents think I should play more" | Now it's about parents, not you |
| Get defensive when coach gives feedback | They'll stop being honest |
| Ask once and never follow up | Shows you weren't serious |
| Expect immediate change | Trust is built over time through action |

---

## Key Principles

1. **Come with curiosity, not complaints** — You're seeking feedback, not arguing your case
2. **Be specific** — "What should I work on?" is better than "Why don't I play?"
3. **Propose action** — Show you're willing to work, not just ask
4. **Follow up** — The conversation means nothing without action and check-in
5. **Accept mistakes** — Tim tells players to make as many mistakes as possible. Embrace that.

---

## For Coaches: How to Handle These Conversations

### What works (from Tim's experience)
- Be specific about what you want to see
- Create safety around mistakes ("I won't sub you for missing shots")
- Give players something concrete to work on
- Acknowledge when they improve (Daniel example)

### What to tell players who ask well
> "Here's the one thing I want you to focus on: [specific skill/behavior]. Show me that in practice, and we'll talk again."

### What to tell players who just want minutes
> "I appreciate you asking. Let me ask you — what do you think you need to work on? ... Okay, show me that consistently in practice, and we'll revisit."

## Success Metrics

**For the player**:
- [ ] Had the conversation (most never do)
- [ ] Got specific feedback
- [ ] Made a commitment to work on something
- [ ] Followed up within 2 weeks
- [ ] Coach noticed effort/improvement

**For the coach**:
- [ ] Player left with clarity on what to work on
- [ ] Player came back and showed effort
- [ ] Relationship strengthened (player trusts they can talk to you)
